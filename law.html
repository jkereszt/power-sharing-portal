Regulatory Foundation

The Energy Sharing Law

§ 42c EnWG explained — what it allows, how it works, and what it means for us.

Source: § 42c EnWG, effective December 23, 2025

The problem the law solves

Germany has more than 5 million rooftop solar installations. Owners who generate more electricity than they use have two options today:

  1. Feed it back to the grid — and receive less than 8 cents per kilowatt-hour, below production cost
  2. Let it go to waste

Meanwhile, their neighbors pay around 30 cents per kilowatt-hour to their energy supplier.

The gap between 8 cents and 30 cents is the market we are entering.

What the law now allows

Since June 1, 2026, grid operators across Germany are legally required to enable Energy Sharing — the direct sale of locally generated renewable electricity from one household to another, through the existing power grid.

No new wires. No new infrastructure. The grid already connects them. What was missing was the legal framework and the accounting system to make it work.

How it works in practice

The electricity does not physically travel from neighbor A to neighbor B in any meaningful sense. The grid is a shared pool. What actually happens is accounting: neighbor A feeds kilowatt-hours into the grid; neighbor B draws kilowatt-hours at the same time. A platform records both, matches them, and settles the financial difference.

This is the same logic used in renewable energy certificates and EV charging networks.

What the law requires

For the solar owner (the "operator")

  • Must be a private individual, partnership, or private entity
  • Members of the community must be end consumers
  • Must operate on a non-commercial basis
  • Can only share electricity from renewable sources
⚠️ Key legal question (OI-001): The "non-commercial basis" requirement may conflict with our platform charging a service fee. This must be resolved by a legal advisor before we finalise the business model. See Risk R-001.

For the community

  • All participants must have smart meters (quarter-hourly measurement)
  • All participants must be within the same local grid area
  • Expands to adjacent grid areas by June 2028

Required contracts (two per participant)

  1. A usage agreement — defining scope, allocation method, and compensation
  2. A power supply contract — covering the energy itself

Mandatory disclosures before signing

  • The solar installation cannot guarantee continuous supply
  • Participants still need a backup electricity supplier
  • Backup supply may cost more than a standard tariff

The allocation question

The law requires every community to define an allocation key — the rule that determines how the solar owner's electricity is distributed among participants. This is one of our core product decisions.

The economics

After adding grid fees, taxes, and concession levies (~10–15 ct/kWh), the numbers look like this:

PartyWithout Energy SharingWith Energy Sharing
Solar owner earns< 8 ct/kWh~15–20 ct/kWh
Neighbor pays~30 ct/kWh~22–27 ct/kWh
⚠️ Risk R-013: The economics are better than today, but the neighbor's saving is smaller than it first appears. Fees and charges absorb much of the benefit. Regulatory reform (virtual balancing areas) is expected to address this.

The strategic implication

The law is real, but its current implementation is technically constrained. Grid operators are not ready, smart meters are not widespread enough, and the economics are thin.

However, a parallel reform — virtual balancing areas — would fix most of these problems and is actively being pushed by citizen energy groups. This model is already proven in EV charging and would allow larger communities, multiple energy sources, and less bureaucracy.
