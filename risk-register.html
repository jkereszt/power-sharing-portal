Legal & Regulatory

R-001 — Revenue model may violate the law ✓ Mitigated

R-001 Low Prob: Low Mitigated

Resolved June 25, 2026. § 42c Abs. 5 explicitly permits professional third-party service providers to operate commercially. The "non-commercial basis" restriction applies to the operator (solar owner), not to us as a platform service provider. We can charge a service fee. The business model is legally sound.

Source: Tillmann Ehlers legal analysis package, June 2026. Previously linked to: OI-001, OI-007

R-002 — Law changes or tightens

Medium Prob: Medium Open

§ 42c EnWG only came into force in December 2025. Implementation guidance is still evolving. The Bundesnetzagentur may issue rules that restrict the market further. Monitor actively.

R-003 — Grid operators not technically ready ✱ Confirmed

High Prob: High Open

Per BDEW internal documents: grid operators will not begin capturing per-connection data until October 1, 2026, with completion optimistically in late 2027. Until then, they can legally terminate Energy Sharing if data is not delivered on time.

Source: Golem.de, June 16, 2026

R-013 — Economics too weak ✱ Confirmed

High Prob: High Open

A solar owner selling at 10 ct/kWh earns more than feed-in rates, but after grid fees, taxes, and surcharges, the neighbor still pays around 25 ct/kWh — barely less than the standard tariff of ~30 ct/kWh. The financial incentive to switch is small.

Source: Golem.de, June 16, 2026

R-014 — Single-source restriction ✱ Partially mitigated

High Prob: High Partial

BDEW internal documents confirm: Energy Sharing is limited to a single generation facility. You cannot combine solar and wind. However, battery storage is possible if the installation meets § 19 Abs. 3b EEG conditions — complex, but viable. EVs remain out of scope. Primary practical case: one PV installation (with optional battery) shared with a few households.

Source: BDEW internal documents via Golem.de; partial mitigation via Tillmann Ehlers, June 2026

R-015 — Law may have been deliberately designed to be unworkable

Medium Prob: Medium Open

Analyst Andreas Schmitz suggests the law may have been shaped by incumbent lobbying — energy suppliers and grid operators have strong incentives against Energy Sharing. Hanlon's Razor also applies: it may simply be incompetence rather than intent.

R-016 — WeShareEnergy and future competitors have a supplier licence head start

Medium Prob: Low Open

WeShareEnergy built Energy Sharing on top of an existing electricity supply licence. § 42c Abs. 5 currently exempts our third-party service provider model from the full supplier obligation catalogue — but if regulators tighten the scope of that exemption, or if our planned operations ever cross the threshold, we face a costly licensed step they already cleared. See OI-015.

Owner: Tillmann/Affan — stress-test the § 42c Abs. 5 exemption boundary

R-017 — Second-wave regulation could tighten or expand rules unpredictably

Medium Prob: Medium Open

When parliament passed § 42c, the committee simultaneously passed a resolution (Entschließung) calling for further rules on Kundenanlagen and improved grid connection procedures. This is a mandated second wave of regulation — changes could simplify grid connection (favorable) or impose stricter obligations (unfavorable). Distinct from R-002, which covers direct amendment to § 42c itself.

Owner: Tillmann — monitor Kundenanlagen proceedings and Bundesnetzagentur guidance

Market & Competition

R-004 — Incumbents launch competing products

High Prob: High Open

E.ON, RWE, EnBW, and large solar providers like Enpal all have the capability to build this. Enpal alone has 500,000+ installed solar systems and direct customer relationships. If they prioritise Energy Sharing, their distribution advantage is very significant.

R-005 — Slow market adoption

Medium Prob: Medium Open

People may not want to manage energy contracts with their neighbors, even if the economics are attractive. UX must minimise friction to near zero. Behavioral barriers are real.

R-006 — Smart meter penetration too low ✱ Confirmed

High Prob: High Open

Smart meters are legally required for all participants. Most German homes do not yet have one. Grid operators begin rollout in October 2026, with optimistic completion in late 2027. This limits near-term addressable market.

Source: Golem.de, June 16, 2026

Product & Technology

R-007 — Grid operator fragmentation ✱ Confirmed

High Prob: High Open

Germany has 800–900 grid and metering operators, each with different systems. Many are still working on their own digitisation. All data collection and billing coordination currently sits with them, with no standardised interface.

Source: Golem.de, June 16, 2026

R-008 — Smart meter data access restricted by GDPR

Medium Prob: Medium Open

Energy consumption data is sensitive personal data. Our data architecture must be GDPR-compliant from day one, which may limit what we can access, store, or process without explicit consent.

R-009 — Prototype scope creep

Medium Prob: Medium Open

With many interesting problems to solve, there is a risk of building too broadly before validating core assumptions. MVP discipline is essential.

Owner: Janos
