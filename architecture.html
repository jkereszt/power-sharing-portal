Platform Architecture

Why Energy Sharing is fundamentally an accounting problem — and how we build the solution.

Last updated: June 21, 2026

The key insight

"Energy Sharing is a bookkeeping exercise, not an engineering problem."

We do not move electrons. We move value. This insight shapes everything about how we build the platform.

The physical reality

Electrons from a rooftop solar panel do not travel to a specific neighbor's house. They feed into the local grid and are consumed by whoever is drawing power at that moment. The grid does not know, and does not care, who "owns" which electrons.

What we can track is:

  • At time T, solar owner A fed X kilowatt-hours into the grid
  • At time T, neighbor B drew Y kilowatt-hours from the grid
  • Our platform records both, matches them by the agreed allocation rule, and settles the financial difference

This is identical to how renewable energy certificates, EV charging roaming, and banking work. The money in your account is not the same physical notes you deposited — but the accounting is real and the value is real.

The four things our platform does

  1. Ingest

    Collect smart meter readings from every participant in the community — every 15 minutes, as required by the law. This is the hardest technical problem: Germany has 800–900 grid operators, each with different systems.

  2. Match

    Apply the community's allocation key to attribute the solar owner's production to specific consumers. Who gets credited with what share of today's sunshine?

  3. Settle

    Calculate financial flows. Generate invoices. Handle the contract obligations between parties automatically.

  4. Report

    Deliver the required data to grid operators to satisfy regulatory obligations.

The OSI model analogy

For those familiar with how the internet works: our platform sits at the application layer. We do not build or operate the infrastructure below us — we build on top of it.

LayerEnergy equivalentWho owns it
Physical (L1)Electricity, wires, the gridGrid operators

Network (L3)Routing across grid areasGrid operators

Important limitation: Unlike internet packets, electrons cannot be addressed or routed to a specific recipient. This analogy is useful for explaining our architecture — not for designing it.

Where our value is defensible

We are not building infrastructure. Our moat is built on four capabilities that get stronger with scale:

CapabilityWhy it's defensible
Data aggregationConnecting and normalising data across hundreds of fragmented grid operators. Harder the more operators we support.
Matching logicThe allocation key algorithm that fairly distributes energy. Improves with every community we run.
Settlement automationContracts, invoices, and financial flows handled without manual intervention. Deeply embedded once live.
Regulatory complianceThe reporting layer that satisfies legal obligations automatically. Becomes a barrier to entry as law evolves.

The bigger opportunity

The current § 42c EnWG law limits Energy Sharing to a single energy source — one solar installation per community. A likely regulatory reform called virtual balancing areas (Virtuelle Bilanzierungsgebiete) would remove this restriction and allow:

  • Multiple sources per community (solar + wind + battery storage)
  • Larger geographic communities
  • Integration of EV batteries and home storage

This reform is technically proven — it is already used in EV charging — and actively supported by citizen energy groups. If and when it passes, the platform we are building becomes the infrastructure layer for a substantially larger market.

One sentence for investors: "We are a fintech platform for energy — we don't move electrons, we move value."
